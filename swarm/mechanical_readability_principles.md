# Mechanical Readability Principles

## Core Standard

The player should see the mechanic.

Text may name a part or gently frame an action, but the lesson should come from visible cause/effect.

## Teach Through Visible Forces

### Visible Force Transfer

When the player applies force, something connected should move.

Examples:

- Brake lever pulls cable.
- Pump handle pushes air.
- Hand pressure compresses tire.
- Pedal rotation pulls chain.

### Visible Motion Transfer

Movement should travel through the mechanism.

Examples:

- Pedal moves chain.
- Chain moves sprocket.
- Sprocket moves wheel.
- Wheel stops when brake contacts rim/rotor.

### Visible Tension

Tension should look like tension.

Examples:

- Brake cable tightens.
- Chain straightens onto teeth.
- Loose chain slack reduces after seating.

### Visible Compression

Compression should deform or close something.

Examples:

- Tire squishes under thumb pressure.
- Brake caliper closes.
- Patch presses flat.
- Suspension compresses and rebounds.

### Visible Friction

Friction should slow or stop motion.

Examples:

- Brake pad contact slows wheel.
- Chain noise quiets when aligned.
- Patch seal stops air leak.

### Visible Repair Progression

Repair should not only be a completed state. The player should see a before, during, and after.

Examples:

- Slipped chain -> guided alignment -> seated chain -> smooth test spin.
- Soft tire -> tube removed -> patch pressed -> tire inflated -> pressure verified.
- Loose brake -> caliper adjusted -> wheel stop verified.

## Prompt Rules

Prompts should invite physical action, not replace it.

Good:

- `Hold Brake`
- `Spin Wheel`
- `Press Patch`
- `Pump Air`
- `Turn Pedal`

Risky:

- `Inspect`
- `Confirm`
- `Tell`
- `Continue`
- `Report`

If a prompt is abstract, the interaction should make the physical meaning obvious.

## Feedback Rules

Feedback hierarchy:

1. Mechanical response.
2. Tactile sound.
3. Small visual confirmation.
4. Short text only if needed.

Avoid making text the primary proof of success.

## Reward Rules

Completion should follow verification.

The player should not receive a reward because they pressed through the correct prompts. They should receive a reward because they tested and saw the bike work.

Best payoff examples:

- Smooth wheel spin.
- Quiet chain movement.
- Brake stops the wheel.
- Tire holds pressure.

## Anatomy Rules

Part names should be learned through use.

The game should say “brake lever” when the player is pressing the lever, not as a floating glossary. Anatomy labels should appear close to the moving part and disappear when they are no longer useful.

## Restraint Rules

Mechanical readability does not require more UI.

Prefer:

- animation
- deformation
- motion
- contact
- sound
- small labels

Avoid:

- quiz overlays
- long tutorials
- reward popups as proof
- constant labels
- large educational panels

## Authorship Standard

The best interaction feels like a child learning from a neighbor at dusk: someone points to the bike, the player tries the thing, and the machine itself explains what happened.
